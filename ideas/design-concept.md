Synthesizes learnings from [[8 Best Academic Lab Websites — Impact Media Lab|8 lab site analyses]], [[Gribble 2025 — How to design your academic website|Gribble (2025)]], and the approved Variant 4 mockup (black + red + Instrument Serif + course-dropdown).

## What makes your lab different (from your promotion dossier)

Your promotion documents give us the identity, voice, and evidence base. The overview brief states it in one line: **"translational statistical science with a clear line of sight to practical impact."** The agent-rules reinforce: "Make teaching, research, and citizenship feel mutually reinforcing rather than compartmentalized." The draft-writer-profile defines the voice: "controlled-strong; active voice preferred; avoid hedging tokens."

That's the website's personality too. Not a list of papers. Not a CV. A **translational bridge** where teaching, research, and citizenship reinforce each other and the evidence is deployment, not citations.

### Voice (from draft-writer-profile)

The promotion dossier's writing profile specifies a voice that should carry to the website:
- **Controlled-strong** — no hedging ("may," "might," "perhaps")
- **Active voice** — "drove," "steered," "anchored," "built," "redesigned," "led"
- **Direct claims** — no "This matters because..." scaffolding
- **Specific, not generic** — concrete numbers, named projects, real outcomes
- **Human and reflective, not bureaucratic** — from agent-rules: "Confident without sounding self-congratulatory"
- **No prestige-by-association** — from agent-rules: avoid name-dropping institutions as a substitute for evidence

This means the website copy should read like the promotion overview, not like a marketing page. Declarative. Evidence-backed. No filler.

### Content tagging strategy

The promotion dossier organises evidence by criteria (TS1, TS3, TS6, TS7 / RE1, RE4, RE7-RE10 / AC2, AC14, AC15). The website should borrow this structure for content tagging — each research stream, publication, and news item gets tagged with its translational outcome:

| Tag | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| `patent` | Granted or pending IP | VectraDA (US 9,200,324) |
| `policy` | Influenced national policy or guidelines | 30,000 Voices → NZ KPIs |
| `product` | Deployed in a commercial product | VectraDA 1M+ patients, WyldBloom clinic pilot |
| `guideline` | Adopted into clinical guidelines | Follicular decline model → Canadian Health Service |
| `teaching` | Curriculum innovation | 161762 interactive PCA widgets |
| `cohort` | Cross-institutional supervision | Round-robin Massey/Auckland cohort |
| `enterprise` | Commercial venture | WyldBloom CEO, MBIE $921K |
| `equity` | Health equity focus | Reihaneh Hosseini: AI-driven breast cancer screening equity |

Publications on the site should show these tags inline — not just title + journal, but `patent` `policy` `product` where applicable. This is how the site shows the translational arc, not just a publication list.

## Proposed concept: "Clinical Realism"

### Core idea
Every element of the site communicates one thing: **methods that survive contact with real clinical data**. Not idealized. Not textbook. The messiness is the point.

### What this means in practice

**Visual identity** (already approved in Variant 4):
- Black `#0a0a0a` — the rigour, the seriousness, the absence of distraction
- Red `#e63946` — urgency, translational impact, clinical relevance (also subtly breast cancer awareness without being literal pink)
- Instrument Serif italic headings — approachable, human, editorial. Not cold tech. Signals "a person wrote this" not "a startup generated this"
- Warm gold `#e9c46a` for `<strong>` emphasis — the human warmth underneath the rigour

**Research framed as clinical questions** (learned from Campbell-Staton, Barrett, Yeşilbaş):

| Current framing | Clinical Realism framing |
|---|---|
| Breast Cancer Risk Models | Can a risk model survive messy screening data? |
| Reproductive AI / Embryo Selection | Can machine learning rank embryos better than an embryologist? |
| Endocrine Therapy Adherence | Can we detect adherence failure before it becomes recurrence? |
| Reproducible Research Methods | Can a method be trusted if no one can reproduce it? |

Each research stream on the homepage shows the **translational arc**: clinical problem → statistical method → where it landed (patent, policy, product, guideline).

**Teaching as toolkit** (already designed in Variant 4):
- Course dropdown sidebar — select a course, materials drop down inline
- Philosophy front and centre: "If a method can't survive messy data, it doesn't belong in the toolkit"
- Each course shows: what you'll learn, how it's assessed, learning outcomes, hosted materials
- Distinctive navigation (learned from Lab to Land's side nav, Sears' species-as-nav)

**People surfaced** (learned from Bhamla, Gribble article):
- Current PhD students with their research questions, not just names
- Alumni and where they landed — shows the pipeline produces real scientists
- This is recruitment infrastructure (Gribble: "the most compelling sites put people up front")

**Credibility on homepage** (learned from Bhamla's 16 media clips, Crowther's UN endorsement):
- 2 US patents granted — not buried in CV, on the homepage stats panel
- Grant reviewer roles (NHMRC MRFF, HRC BMAC) — signals you judge what good evidence looks like
- 30,000 Voices defined ~50% of NZ's national breast cancer KPIs — system change, not a publication
- VectraDA run on 1M+ patients globally — methods that left the building
- WyldBloom: patent-pending, MBIE-funded, clinic-pilot stage

**Homepage stats panel (revised — deployment evidence, not input metrics):**
- 2 — US patents granted
- 1M+ — patients monitored with VectraDA
- ~50% — of NZ's breast cancer KPIs shaped by 30,000 Voices
- 2 — institutions (Massey + Auckland, one integrated cohort)

**Recruitment as first-class** (learned from Barrett's nav button, Bhamla's homepage section):
- "Join the Lab" in nav
- Mentorship philosophy stated explicitly
- What to send (CV, statement, transcript) — not "contact us if interested"
- Shared values — what it's like to work in this lab

**News feed** (learned from Bhamla, Campbell-Staton, Crowther):
- Recent papers, grants, student milestones, conference talks
- Keeps the site alive (Gribble: "an outdated site is counterproductive")

**WyldBloom link** (already designed):
- Cyan-tinted button in nav — visually distinct, signals external entity
- Footer link in Company column
- The relationship is clear: WyldBloom is where the embryo selection research became a product

**Imagery** (biggest gap in our current mockup — learned from all 8 sites):
- Embryo microscopy images (morphokinetic stage detection)
- Breast imaging visualizations (multi-modality screening)
- Data plots from papers (not decorative — actual results)
- No stock photos. No generic "scientist at microscope" shots.
- If you have any recorded talks/presentations, embed one (learned from Sears' CBC clip, Campbell-Staton's TED talk)

## Tagline options

Current: "Statistical and machine learning systems for clinically grounded translational research" — too long, too generic, could be anyone.

The 8 lab sites taught me: the best taglines are 4-6 words, state a position, and couldn't belong to another lab.

Barrett's: "Boldly empirical. Radically compassionate."
Campbell-Staton's: "Exploring human activity as a force for evolutionary change."
Yeşilbaş's: "Using spectroscopy to search for life beyond Earth."

Here are 5 for you, all centred on the identity from your promotion dossier: **translational statistical science with a clear line of sight to practical impact.**

### 1. "Statistical methods with a line of sight to clinical impact."
Your own words from the promotion overview brief. The phrase you already chose to describe yourself. It says what you do (statistical methods), how you work (line of sight — not blind translation, deliberate aim), and where it lands (clinical impact). NZ spelling, declarative, no hedging.

### 2. "Methods that survive messy data."
Your teaching philosophy, already in the mockup. Shorter, punchier, more provocative. A challenge to the field. But it foregrounds the mess, not the impact — the line of sight is implied, not stated.

### 3. "From statistical problem to clinical decision."
The translational arc in 6 words. Says exactly what happens: problem in, decision out. The "line of sight" is the arrow between the two. Not aspiration — description.

### 4. "Statistics for the data you actually have."
Anti-idealized. Rejects the textbook. Speaks to students, clinicians, and funders simultaneously. But it's about the input side, not the impact side — the line of sight runs one way.

### 5. "Built for the mess. Proven in the clinic."
Two-beat structure: philosophy then evidence. "Proven" is strong — you have patents and deployments to back it. The line of sight is the period between the two sentences.

## My recommendation

**Tagline: "Statistical methods with a line of sight to clinical impact."**

It's your own identity statement from the promotion dossier. It's specific (statistical methods, not "science" or "research"), directional (line of sight — aimed, not accidental), and outcome-focused (clinical impact, not publications). No other lab would use this exact phrasing because no other lab has the deployment evidence to make "line of sight" credible rather than aspirational.

**Concept: Clinical Realism** — every design decision should answer: does this communicate that your methods work in the real world, not just in papers?
